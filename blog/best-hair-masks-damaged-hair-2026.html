Types of Hair Damage and What Fixes Them

Not all hair damage is the same, and using the wrong type of mask can actually make things worse. Understanding your damage type is the first step to effective treatment.

Bleach and Chemical Damage

Bleaching breaks the disulfide bonds within the hair cortex, which are the structural bonds that give hair its strength and shape. This type of damage requires bond repair treatments that reconnect these broken bonds at the molecular level. Olaplex pioneered this technology, and several brands now offer effective bond-building formulas. Symptoms: extreme dryness, rubber-band-like stretching when wet, breakage, loss of curl pattern.

Heat Damage

Flat irons, curling irons, and blow dryers above 300 degrees Fahrenheit (150 degrees Celsius) melt the keratin proteins that form the hair shaft. Heat damage is technically irreversible at the structural level, but protein treatments can temporarily fill in the damaged areas, restoring strength and manageability until the damaged sections grow out. Symptoms: hair does not hold curl or style, rough texture, split ends, lack of shine.

Moisture Loss (Hygral Fatigue)

Hair that is chronically dry, exposed to hard water, sun, wind, or overwashed loses its moisture balance. The cuticle layer lifts, allowing internal moisture to escape. Deep conditioning with moisture-rich masks restores hydration and smooths the cuticle. Symptoms: frizz, tangles, dull appearance, rough feel, static.

How to Use a Hair Mask Properly

Step 1: Shampoo First

Always apply hair masks to freshly shampooed, towel-dried hair (unless the product specifically directs otherwise). Shampoo opens the hair cuticle and removes product buildup, allowing the mask's ingredients to penetrate effectively. Skip conditioner before the mask; the mask replaces conditioner for that wash.

Step 2: Apply Generously to Mid-Lengths and Ends

Focus the mask on the mid-lengths and ends of your hair, which are the oldest and most damaged sections. Avoid applying heavy masks to the roots, which can weigh hair down and cause greasiness. Use a wide-tooth comb to distribute the product evenly through the hair.

Step 3: Wait the Full Recommended Time

Do not rush. Most masks need 5 to 20 minutes to work. For deeper penetration, cover your hair with a shower cap and apply gentle heat (a warm towel or hooded dryer). Heat opens the cuticle further, allowing deeper ingredient penetration. The difference between a 3-minute and a 15-minute treatment is significant.

Step 4: Rinse with Cool Water

Rinse thoroughly with cool or lukewarm water. Cool water helps seal the cuticle, locking in the mask's benefits. Hot water opens the cuticle and can wash away the conditioning agents you just spent time depositing.

The Protein-Moisture Balance

Healthy hair requires a balance of protein and moisture. Too much protein makes hair stiff, brittle, and prone to snapping. Too little protein makes hair mushy, stretchy, and limp. Alternate between protein and moisture masks on different weeks to maintain the optimal balance. If your hair feels straw-like after a protein treatment, follow with a deep moisture mask the next wash.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should you use a hair mask?

For damaged hair, use a deep conditioning mask once per week. For severely damaged or bleached hair, twice per week is appropriate for the first month, then reduce to weekly maintenance. Overusing protein masks can cause protein overload, making hair stiff and brittle, so alternate between protein and moisture masks.

What is the difference between a hair mask and conditioner?

Regular conditioner smooths the hair cuticle and provides surface-level softness for daily use, typically left on for 1 to 3 minutes. Hair masks contain higher concentrations of reparative ingredients like proteins, oils, and bond-building agents, and are left on for 5 to 30 minutes for deeper penetration. Masks repair damage while conditioners maintain hair between treatments.

Do bond repair hair masks actually work?

Yes. Bond repair technology, pioneered by Olaplex, uses bis-aminopropyl diglycol dimaleate and similar molecules to reconnect broken disulfide bonds within the hair cortex. Clinical studies show measurable improvement in hair strength, elasticity, and breakage reduction after consistent use. The results are cumulative, meaning regular use provides ongoing improvement.

Should I use a protein mask or moisture mask?

It depends on your hair's specific damage type. Hair that stretches excessively when wet and feels mushy needs protein. Hair that snaps easily when pulled and feels straw-like needs moisture. Most damaged hair benefits from alternating between protein and moisture masks on different weeks to maintain the protein-moisture balance.

Can you leave a hair mask on overnight?

Some hair masks are formulated for overnight use, and these are safe to leave on while you sleep (use a silk pillowcase or shower cap). However, protein-heavy masks should never be left on overnight as this can cause protein overload, leaving hair stiff, dry, and prone to breakage. Always check the product instructions. When in doubt, limit mask time to 30 minutes.
